Potential Flow

Irrotational: \(\nabla \times \vec{V} = 0\)

\[\Rightarrow \boxed{\vec{V} = \nabla \phi}\]

Incompressible: \(\nabla \cdot \vec{V} = 0\)

\[\nabla \cdot \nabla \phi = 0\] \[\boxed{\nabla^2 \phi = 0}\]

This is Laplace's Equation!

Velocity Field

\[ \vec{V} = \nabla \phi \quad\Rightarrow\quad V_x = \frac{\partial \phi}{\partial x}, V_y = \frac{\partial \phi}{\partial y}, V_z = \frac{\partial \phi}{\partial z} \]

\[ \vec{V} = \nabla \times \psi \hat{z} \quad\Rightarrow\quad V_x = \frac{\partial \psi}{\partial y}, V_y = -\frac{\partial \psi}{\partial x}, V_z = 0 \]

Uniform Flow

\[ \phi = U \cos\alpha x + U \sin\alpha y \]

\[ \psi = -U \sin\alpha x + U \cos\alpha y \]

\[ V_x = U \cos\alpha\]

\[ V_y = U \sin\alpha\]

Panel Methods

We want to be able to simulate potential flow over arbitrary complex geometries (and not just spheres and ovals).

How can we achieve this?

  • Place known solutions (e.g., vortices, doublets) on the surface of our geometry. This automatically satisfies the governing equation.
  • Only need to satisfy boundary condition. Freestream boundaries are satisfied automatically (why?). Our only task is to adjust strengths of singularities to satisfy flow tangency.

We can use either continuous or discontinuous singularities. Usually we discretize our geometry into panels, thus the name panel methods. The result is a linear system of equations, which is easily solvable.
