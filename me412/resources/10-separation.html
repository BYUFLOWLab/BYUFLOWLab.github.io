Transition

Instabilities in the boundary layer lead to transition.

For low Reynolds numbers, instabilities are damped and the flow remains laminar.

At high Reynolds numbers, instabilities are amplified leading to turbulent flow.

We often think of transition occurring at a specific point, but it really occurs over a region.

How can we predict transition?

Empirical Methods

Michel's criteria (for airfoils in incompressible flow):

\[Re_\theta > 1.174 \left( 1 + \frac{22,400}{Re_x} Re_x^{0.46} \right)\]

Linear Stability Theory

A popular approach is the \(e^n\) method (used in XFOIL).

Tracks frequencies in the Orr-Sommerfeld equation and sets transition when the fastest growing frequency is amplified by a factor of \(e^n\) where \(n\) is a user chosen constant.

\(n_{cric} = 9\) is a typical value (but depends on the flow conditions) and means transition is predicted to occur when an initial perturbation has grown by a factor of \(e^9 = 8103\).

3D transition

These 2D instabilities (called Tollmein-Schlichting instabilities) are not the only mechanism that cause transition. 3D affects are also important (attachment line instabilities, crossflow instabilities).

What factors can we change to affect transition?

  • Freestream turbulence and noise
  • Pressure gradients: even very small adverse pressure gradients can cause transition (more on this next)
  • Heat transfer: cooling can stabilize a boundary layer
  • Suction/blowing
  • Surface roughness: sometimes intentional trips are added

Separation

Pressure Gradients

Recall that the pressure gradient normal to the surface is small. However, in the streamwise direction the pressure gradient is generally nonzero.

Favorable and Adverse Pressure Gradients

An adverse pressure gradient decreases the fluid momentum (like coasting uphill). At some point the streamlines leave the surface and this causes separation. Downstream of separation there are flow reversals.

\[\left(\frac{\partial u}{\partial y}\right)_{\text{wall}} = 0\]

Turbulent flows have higher velocities closer to the surface and thus are more resistant to separation.

Separation generally leads to a massive increase in drag, and a decrease in lift.

Conversely, a favorable pressure gradient can stabilize a boundary layer.

Want to avoid stagnation points in an adverse gradient (hence fairings).

Once separation occurs, a flow may reattach.
