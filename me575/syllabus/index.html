Objective

To engage in deep learning of optimization principles, be able to effectively setup and solve real-world optimization problems, and develop technical and communication skills to prepare you to be amongst the world’s most influential engineers.

Overview

Optimization consists of finding the best feasible selection amongst competing alternatives. Optimization methods are used in a wide range of applications including:

  • design: designing the shape of a car for minimum drag
  • controls: selecting the best actuation sequence of a robot to minimize expended energy
  • resource allocation: deciding what quantities of material to send to which stores to maximize production
  • trajectory planning: finding the best path for a UAV to maximize its observable area
  • regression: finding the best curve parameters to describe the data
  • physics simulation: finding the shape of a hanging chain that minimizes potential energy
  • machine learning: determining model parameters to predict Netflix movies you are likely to be interested in
  • and many others…

There is no one optimization algorithm to rule them all, so in this class we will learn about several different classes of algorithms. You will learn the strengths and limitations of different approaches, and how to frame your analysis in a way that is “optimization-friendly”. Optimization almost always involves complicated tradeoffs, and computers are useful in managing this complexity and extracting insight.

What you take away from the course will greatly depend on your attitude, diligence, initiative, and willingness to work with and learn from others.

Prerequisites

  • multivariable calculus
  • linear algebra
  • computer programming

This course assumes no prior exposure to optimization, but you will need a foundation in mathematics and programming.

What math concepts should I review?

The main concepts you should review from multivariable calculus are partial derivatives, the chain rule, directional derivatives, and the gradient for multivariable functions. For linear algebra here is a good review. Almost every concept in this PDF is one we will use at some point this semester.

How much programming experience do I need to take this class?

You should be familiar and comfortable with the basics: functions, loops, conditionals, variable types, arrays (vectors), use of numerical libraries. Familiarity with object oriented programming is not necessary. It does not matter too much what language(s) you have experience with as long as you understand the fundamentals. This year we will use Matlab. The concepts you learn in this course are translatable to other programming languages or optimization packages.

While this is not a programming course, many of the concepts are best learned through implementation. Some of these algorithms are involved and optimization methods are great at exploiting any errors or weakness in implementation. You will need to develop and refine your skills in code debugging as well as in physical insight. I do not recommend taking this class if you do not have programming experience. It will be very difficult to get up to speed with programming and stay on top of the optimization concepts at the same time. Note that programming experience need not necessarily come from a formal course.

Can I take this class as an undergraduate?

Definitely. Many undergraduates elect to take this course and do very well, but keep in mind that this is a graduate course and expectations will be consistent with graduate-level work. Also, the course project is often (but not always) most effective when applied to an ongoing research project. If you are not involved in research, you may want to join a team with a graduate student. As compared to an undergraduate class:

  • the workload is higher (more time required).
  • the problems are more difficult and require more open-ended problem solving and debugging skills.
  • the homeworks are more like reports—you need to explain your methodology and results and not just “circle your answer”.
  • you need to be more of an independent learner (set your own reading schedule, break up larger assignments and projects, find and use outside resources, etc.).
  • the required level of mathematical understanding is higher.

Learning Outcomes

1. Define and Use Optimization Terminology and Concepts

Define and use optimization terminology and concepts, and understand how to classify an optimization problem.

2. Application of Optimization Methods to Engineering Problems

Apply optimization methods to engineering problems, including developing a model, defining an optimization problem, applying optimization methods, exploring the solution, and interpreting results. Students will demonstrate the ability to choose and justify optimization techniques that are appropriate for solving realistic engineering problems.

3. Unconstrained Optimization Theory for Continuous Problems

Understand and apply unconstrained optimization theory for continuous problems, including the necessary and sufficient optimality conditions and algorithms such as: steepest descent, Newton’s method, conjugate gradient, and quasi-Newton methods.

4. Computing Derivatives for Gradient-based Optimization

Understand and apply methods for computing derivatives such as: finite differencing, symbolic differentiation, complex step, algorithmic differentiation, and analytic methods (direct and adjoint).

5. Gradient-free and Discrete Optimization Algorithms

Understand and apply gradient-free and discrete optimization algorithms such as: genetic algorithms, particle swarm optimization, branch and bound, exhaustive search, and simulated annealing.

6. Constrained Optimization Theory for Continuous Problems

Understand and apply constrained optimization theory for continuous problems, including the Karush-Kuhn-Tucker conditions and algorithms such as: generalized reduced gradient, sequential quadratic programming, and interior-point methods.

7. Determining a Robust Design

Apply optimization techniques to determine a robust design.

8. Optimization Software

Be able to implement basic optimization algorithms in a computational setting and apply existing optimization software packages to solve engineering problems.

Course Work

A higher workload is expected in graduate courses as compared to undergradaute courses. As a rule of thumb you should plan on four hours of work per week per credit hour. Subtracting class time, that means at least 9.5 hours of work outside of class each week.

Participation

Good: attend class regularly and punctually.
Better: attend and be attentive in class.
Best: attend, be attentive, and actively participate in the classroom discussion.

Don’t be afraid to ask what you think might be a simple question, usually someone else is wondering the same thing and you are doing them a favor by asking. Be respectful of your peers and arrive to class on time (but if you are late please don’t feel like you can’t come in). You may use electronic devices in the classroom only if they are being used appropriately for class activities (e.g., taking notes).

Class time should not be a passive experience. In addition to asking questions, you should come prepared to work on problems in class. I will often ask you to work individually or with small groups on example problems in class. Many of these problems will be ones that you haven’t seen and don’t know how to solve. That may be uncomfortable at first, but this practice will help you to learn concepts more deeply and become better engineering problem solvers.

I provide slides for each lecture. They are intentionally sparse. Studies suggest that you will learn and retain information better by taking notes, so I intentionally avoid providing you with detailed notes. On the other hand, requiring you to copy down lots of information won’t give you space to think in class. The goal of the slides is to hopefully reach a happy medium. The lecture slides are used to provide organization and display items that would be time consuming to write down (e.g., images, long equations). Important details are elaborated on in class, and it is expected that you will be taking good notes. Some students like to use the slides as a background to write their notes on.

Homework

There are six homeworks designed to teach the important concepts in the class and to help you succeed on your project and future optimization problems. Each homework will be assigned two weeks before its due date and will be submitted in the form of a short report via Learning Suite. Homework will typically be due every Wednesday at midnight (actually 11:59pm) and will be turned in via Learning Suite. Homework submission guidelines are linked from the schedule page (direct link). Late homework will be accepted with a penalty of 25% per day. Last minute issues often happen in life so be sure to start your work early and submit early!

Homework will be primarily self-graded. I will provided detailed solutions/guides, and you will assess your own work. Reviewing your own work with a guided solution will help you better understand concepts you may have missed, and will allow you to see alternative solution approaches. Mistakes can be some of our most effective teachers, but to take advantage of the learning opportunity we must review our work, figure out what the mistake was, and learn how to address it. The grader will spot check certain problems to help ensure grading is applied appropriately and consistently, and to provide feedback on things where more experience is required.

Working with others on homework is certainly encouraged, but you must do your own work and prepare your own reports. Use of solutions, homework, or exams from prior students or other sources is a violation of the honor code.

Project

The main thrust of this course is a semester-long project that will allow you to dive deeper into an area or application that you are interested in. The project is open-ended and you will work in small groups of size 2-3. The end product will be a report (of typical journal paper length) appropriately formatted and describing the problem, your methodology, the results, and your conclusions. Excellent projects will result in publishable or nearly-publishable work.

Your project may be aligned with research you are doing outside of the classroom (in fact I recommend doing so if possible). The milestones within the project will consist of a short abstract due early in the semester, an extended outline due about a month before the end of the semester, an in-class presentation, and a final report due on the last day of class. More details on the project are found here. Example project reports from past classes can be found on the Resources section.

Advice

Each homework is assigned two weeks before the deadline because it is designed to take that long. Plan your time well. If you put off most of the work until a few days before the deadline you will not be as successful. Some of you will struggle with the scheduling format because you are used to homeworks and readings being divided into smaller chunks for you. This is a graduate class and I expect you to be able to do your own task management. As you prepare for the workplace this is a critical skill to develop.

Debugging is one of the most challenging parts of optimization. Your level of skill with debugging will often be correlated with how long it takes you to complete the homeworks. There are two aspects of debugging in this context: coding implementation and problem formulation.

The first aspect to debugging is to make sure your code is implemented correctly. This challenge is common to any programming work. I suggest that you start simple and test frequently. Even though it may seem slower to stop and test as you go, it will save you lots of time in the end. Break algorithms into small pieces. Determine what the expected output should be for a small piece and thoroughly test it before adding more functionality. Test boundary cases as well. Learn how to step line by line through your code, plot outputs, etc. Formalizing this process is called unit testing.

The second aspect is to appropriately formulate your optimization problem, particularly in your project. Is your objective function suitable? Is there a better higher-level objective? Should your objective actually be a constraint? Are there other important constraints that you have not considered? What do you expect the solution to be? How does the starting point affect your solution? Have you explored the scaling of the problem? Can you decompose your problem into a smaller problem to build on (i.e., start with just a few design variables at first, remove some constraints). Have you explored the behavior of all outputs with respect to all inputs? Did you provide gradients, and if so did you run a gradient check? Have you looked at the output messages from the optimizer?

Plagiarism

Intentional plagiarism is a form of intellectual theft that violates widely recognized principles of academic integrity as well as the Honor Code. Such plagiarism may subject the student to appropriate disciplinary action administered through the university Honor Code Office, in addition to academic sanctions that may be applied by an instructor. Inadvertent plagiarism, which may not be a violation of the Honor Code, is nevertheless a form of intellectual carelessness that is unacceptable in the academic community. Plagiarism of any kind is completely contrary to the established practices of higher education where all members of the university are expected to acknowledge the original intellectual work of others that is included in their own work. In some cases, plagiarism may also involve violations of copyright law. Intentional Plagiarism-Intentional plagiarism is the deliberate act of representing the words, ideas, or data of another as one’s own without providing proper attribution to the author through quotation, reference, or footnote. Inadvertent Plagiarism-Inadvertent plagiarism involves the inappropriate, but non-deliberate, use of another’s words, ideas, or data without proper attribution. Inadvertent plagiarism usually results from an ignorant failure to follow established rules for documenting sources or from simply not being sufficiently careful in research and writing. Although not a violation of the Honor Code, inadvertent plagiarism is a form of academic misconduct for which an instructor can impose appropriate academic sanctions. Students who are in doubt as to whether they are providing proper attribution have the responsibility to consult with their instructor and obtain guidance. Examples of plagiarism include: Direct Plagiarism-The verbatim copying of an original source without acknowledging the source. Paraphrased Plagiarism-The paraphrasing, without acknowledgement, of ideas from another that the reader might mistake for the author’s own. Plagiarism Mosaic-The borrowing of words, ideas, or data from an original source and blending this original material with one’s own without acknowledging the source. Insufficient Acknowledgement-The partial or incomplete attribution of words, ideas, or data from an original source. Plagiarism may occur with respect to unpublished as well as published material. Copying another student’s work and submitting it as one’s own individual work without proper attribution is a serious form of plagiarism.
