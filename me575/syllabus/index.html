Syllabus

Overview

Optimization consists of finding the best feasible selection amongst competing alternatives. Optimization methods are used in a wide range of applications including:

  • design–designing the outer shape of a car for minimum drag
  • trajectory planning–finding the best path for a UAV to maximize its observable area
  • controls–selecting the best actuation sequence of a robot to minimize its expended energy
  • resource allocation–deciding what quantities of material to send to which stores to maximize production
  • regression–finding the best curve to describe your data
  • physics simulation–finding the shape of a hanging chain (minimum potential energy)
  • machine learning–finding the best model parameters to predict what Netflix movies you might be interested in based on past ratings
  • and many other areas

There is no one optimization algorithm to rule them all, so in this class we will learn about several different classes of algorithms. You will learn the strengths and limitations of different approaches, and how to frame your analysis in a way that is "optimization-friendly". Optimization almost always involves complicated tradeoffs, and computers are useful in managing this complexity and extracting insight.

Prerequisites

  • multivariable calculus
  • linear algebra
  • computer programming

How much programming experience is needed?

You should be familiar and comfortable with the basics: functions, loops, conditionals, variable types, arrays (vectors). It does not matter too much what language(s) you have experience with as long as you understand the fundamentals. You can use any language you want in this class, but most students elect to use Matlab because the syntax is simple and the optimization toolbox is easily accessible. I will show examples in class using both Matlab and Python. The concepts you learn in this course will translate to other programming languages or optimization packages.

Although the necessary programming concepts are basic, some of the algorithms we will implement are long and complex. In addition, optimization methods are great at exploiting weaknesses in implementation. You will need to develop and refine your skills in code debugging as well as in physical insight. I do not recommend taking this class if you do not have any programming experience. It will be very difficult to get up to speed with programming and stay on top of the optimization concepts at the same time. Programming experience need not necessarily come from a formal course. There are many other resources you can use to gain experience before taking this class.

Course Content

There will be 6 modules in the course each lasting 1-3 weeks. A overview of the topics is listed below.

  1. Introduction and 1D Optimization
    • Basic Concepts and Terminology
    • Optimality Conditions
    • Bisection
    • Newton's Method
    • Wolfe Conditions
  2. Unconstrained Optimization
    • Gradients and Hessians
    • Optimality Conditions
    • Steepest Descent
    • Conjugate Gradient
    • Newton and Quasi-Newton Methods
    • Trust Region Methods
  3. Computing Gradients
    • Finite difference
    • Complex step
    • Automatic differentiation
    • Adjoint and direct methods
  4. Constrained Optimization
    • Optimality Conditions
    • Penalty Methods
    • Sequential Quadratic Programming
    • Interior Point Methods
  5. Gradient-Free Methods
    • Nelder-Mead Simplex
    • Genetic Algorithms
    • Particle Swarm Optimization
    • Multiobjective optimization (for gradient and non-gradient based)
  6. Miscellaneous and Advanced Topics
    • Robust optimization
    • Surrogate-based optimization
    • MDO architectures
    • Integer and mixed integer programming

Course Work

Homework (40%)

There will be six homeworks designed to teach the important concepts in the class. Homeworks will have a 2-week window and will be submitted in the form of a short report. Late homework will not be accepted (except with prior approval for extreme extenuating circumstances). Homework submission details are provided in a supplementary handout.

Project (33%)

The main thrust of this course is a semester-long project that will allow you to dive deeper into an area or application that you are interested in. The project is open-ended and you will work in small groups. The end product will be a report (of typical journal paper length) appropriately formated and describing the problem, your methodology, the results, and your conclusions. Excellent projects will result in publishable or nearly-publishable work. Your project may supplement research/work you are doing outside of the classroom, but you will only receive credit for work done this semester for this class. The milestones within the project will consist of a short abstract due early in the semester, a draft report due about a month before the end of the semester, an in-class presentation, and a final report due on the last day of class. More details on the project are given in a supplementary handout.

Quizes and Exams (27%)

This class will have a midterm and a final exam. In addition there will be several unannounced in-class quizes. The quizes will not be graded for correctness, but will provide you and me with ongoing feedback.

Partipcation includes asking questions and being engaged in the learning process.Don’t be afraid to ask what you think might be a simple question, chances are someone else is wondering the same thing. At the same time, be respectful of your peer's time. If there is a topic that will require a longer conversation seek out the TA or myself outside of class time. I expect you to attend class, be punctual, and be attentive. You may use electronic devices in the classroom only if they are being used appropriately for class activities (e.g., taking notes).

Plagiarism

Intentional plagiarism is a form of intellectual theft that violates widely recognized principles of academic integrity as well as the Honor Code. Such plagiarism may subject the student to appropriate disciplinary action administered through the university Honor Code Office, in addition to academic sanctions that may be applied by an instructor. Inadvertent plagiarism, which may not be a violation of the Honor Code, is nevertheless a form of intellectual carelessness that is unacceptable in the academic community. Plagiarism of any kind is completely contrary to the established practices of higher education where all members of the university are expected to acknowledge the original intellectual work of others that is included in their own work. In some cases, plagiarism may also involve violations of copyright law. Intentional Plagiarism-Intentional plagiarism is the deliberate act of representing the words, ideas, or data of another as one's own without providing proper attribution to the author through quotation, reference, or footnote. Inadvertent Plagiarism-Inadvertent plagiarism involves the inappropriate, but non-deliberate, use of another's words, ideas, or data without proper attribution. Inadvertent plagiarism usually results from an ignorant failure to follow established rules for documenting sources or from simply not being sufficiently careful in research and writing. Although not a violation of the Honor Code, inadvertent plagiarism is a form of academic misconduct for which an instructor can impose appropriate academic sanctions. Students who are in doubt as to whether they are providing proper attribution have the responsibility to consult with their instructor and obtain guidance. Examples of plagiarism include: Direct Plagiarism-The verbatim copying of an original source without acknowledging the source. Paraphrased Plagiarism-The paraphrasing, without acknowledgement, of ideas from another that the reader might mistake for the author's own. Plagiarism Mosaic-The borrowing of words, ideas, or data from an original source and blending this original material with one's own without acknowledging the source. Insufficient Acknowledgement-The partial or incomplete attribution of words, ideas, or data from an original source. Plagiarism may occur with respect to unpublished as well as published material. Copying another student's work and submitting it as one's own individual work without proper attribution is a serious form of plagiarism.
