Flying

Pilot Certification

  1. Simulator Certification. Practice on the simiulator in the Airplane Lab. When ready, pass off to the TA. You will need to use a model with similar controls to yours. The TA will ask you to: take-off, demonstrate well-controlled orbits, and land. These tasks will then be repeated with wind/gusts. Finally, you will need to demonstrate recovery from stalls and spins.
  2. Live Certification. Using a trainer (or your plane) fly with a “buddy box” (controlled by the TA), perform similar tasks as in the simulator. Then perform a solo flight.
  3. Review safety/procedures with the TAs.

Once certified, you are free to checkout the transmitters. Be sure to fill out the check-out sheet.

Pre-Flight Checklist

Safety is the most important consideration. Do not take unnecessary risks.

  1. Check surroundings. Is it safe to fly? Is it windy? Are there too many people around?
  2. Check cg location.
  3. Check that electronic components are secure.
  4. Perform a visual inspection of plane including props and control surface attachments. If adjusting props make sure battery is off.
  5. Turn on transmitter
  6. Make sure throttle is all the way down to zero.
  7. Plug in battery
  8. Check that the propellers are spinning in the correct direction.
  9. Check that the control surfaces move in the expected directions.
  10. Have another team member prepare to launch. Establish a countdown, throttle up, and launch into the wind.

Autopilot

The autopilots are the most expensive component, and building your plane took a lot of time and effort. You reduce the risk of damaging either through a careful progressive testing process.

  • Fly new designs RC first for several flights. Don’t put the autopilot on board. Use the dummy pod (similar size and weight).
  • Test new autopilot algorithms on the ground first. Walk around with your plane and observe control surfaces and throttle settings. Make sure it does what you expect.
  • Simulate RC flights onboard the autopilot. You can record an RC flight and “playback” the flight onboard the computer to observe what the autopilot would do.
