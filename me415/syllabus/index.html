Overview

This course examines fundamental principles for designing flight vehicles (e.g., airplanes and rockets). This includes introductions to aerodynamics, structures, stability, propulsion, and performance. Rather than diving deeply into any one discipline, our focus is on understanding fundamental trade-offs amongst these multidisciplinary considerations to allow one to approach vehicle-level design problems. This class will involve a combination of theory, computational methods, and a hands-on project.

The content of this class is the typical launching point for a mechanical engineering wishing to specialize in aerospace engineering. Subsequent courses provide greater depth on topics that we can only cover at an introductory level: aerodynamics (515), gas turbine and jet engine design (426), compressible fluid flow (510), computational fluid dynamics (541), design optimization (575), composites (456, 602), aircraft structures (523), control systems (431), flight dynamics and control (634).

Prerequisites

Prerequisites: ME EN 273, ME EN 312 (the latter may be taken concurrently)

This course assumes no prior exposure to aircraft or spacecraft, but you will need a solid foundation in engineering fundamentals, namely: numerical methods/programming, fluids, dynamics, and structures.

Learning Outcomes

Aerodynamics: Analyze and measure aerodynamic forces and moments.

Structures: Analyze structural response to aerodynamic and inertial loads.

Stability: Analyze longitudinal and lateral stability both statically and dynamically.

Propulsion: Analyze thrust and propulsive efficiency.

Performance: Select appropriate performance metrics, and evaluate performance.

Integrated Design: Analyze and design a flight vehicle for a specified mission using a well-reasoned and clearly articulated approach.

Participation

Good: attend class regularly and punctually.
Better: attend and be attentive in class.
Best: attend, be attentive, and actively participate in the classroom discussion.

Don’t be afraid to ask what you think might be a simple question, usually someone else is wondering the same thing and you are doing them a favor by asking. Be respectful of your peers and arrive to class on time (but if you are late please don’t feel like you can’t come in).

Class time should not be a passive experience. In addition to asking questions, you should come prepared to work on problems in class. I will often ask you to work individually or with small groups on example problems and design exercises in class. Bring a laptop if possible. If you don’t have access to one, the classroom has some available computers and you can work with others.

Reading

Most class periods will have an assigned reading, and I expect you to complete the readings beforehand. I cannot, not would it be effective to, cover all the content you need to know in class. Instead, class time will be used for discussing difficult concepts, diving deeper into or expanding on concepts you were exposed to in your reading, answering questions, working examples together, and testing understanding.

Readings will come from Introduction to Flight by John Anderson (5th, 6th, 7th, or 8th Ed.). No homework problems will come from the text, just background reading. The differences in these versions are minor and inconsequential for our purposes. Having more options should allow you to save costs. The level of the text is a bit too basic for our needs, but covers a broad range of topics and will be supplemented with more detailed in-class notes and discussion. I will also direct you to supplementary readings for those interested in more depth in particularly topics.

Homework

Homework will typically be due every Wednesday at midnight (actually 11:59pm) and will be turned in via Learning Suite. Late homework will be accepted with a penalty of 20% per day. Last minute issues often happen in life so be sure to start your work early and submit early! Despite your best planning you will likely have an unexpected event, obligation, or illness pop up. To help with this, I will drop your lowest homework score. This policy is designed to help with unexpected issues so please don’t ask for homework extensions or other exceptions (unless you have an extreme event like a hospitalization or death in the family). Homework will largely be design oriented, but will also involve a mix of traditional textbook problems, computational implementation and analysis, and experimental data gathering and processing.

Assignments are expected to be produced in a report or memo format. A series of equations and “solutions” is insufficient. Each report should introduce the problem and any assumptions, detail the methodology, and discuss the results and conclusions. Please see the BYU Mechanical Engineering Department Problem Solving Resources. Specifically, look at Communicating Engineering Solutions. If unfamiliar with IMRaD, information on this writing genre is provided at the same link.

Working with others on homework is certainly encouraged, but you must prepare your own reports. Referring to solution manuals or prior student’s homework is a violation of the honor code.

Influential Engineers

One of the fundamental objectives of the department is to help you become influential engineers. We have drafted the following summary of what it means to be an influential engineer that you should become familiar with.

Influential engineers impact the world in a variety of ways. While some may affect large numbers of people in obvious, publicly recognized ways, others will quietly lead and inspire those within their sphere of influence in less recognized ways.

To maximize their potential to be influential engineers, students must come to recognize their strengths and apply them in ways that lead to a positive impact on people, problems, and the profession. Since each student is unique, defining influence is highly personal and will typically change over time as new opportunities arise and new skills are developed. Regardless of where students find themselves having the most impact, they will become influential engineers as they develop technical excellence, leadership, communication skills, character, and a commitment to lifelong learning and service.

Influential Engineers Develop…

  • Technical Excellence: Influential engineers are innovative experts in their field. They have earned trust from others by successfully apply engineering concepts, principles and practices to solve real world problems..
  • Leadership: Regardless of their positions, influential engineers lead in thought and behavior. They are more focused on the greater good than on personal achievement. They are able to make tough decisions, when necessary. They inspire and help others to reach their potential.
  • Communication Skills: Influential engineers effectively communicate results and conclusions clearly and concisely. They communicate persuasively to present recommendations and prevent and solve problems. They work well with others.
  • Character: Influential engineers are individuals of high integrity. Their character and competence are respected by colleagues. An influential engineer’s sphere of influence is expanded as people come to recognize and trust their character.
  • Lifelong Learning and Service: Influential engineers want to do good in the world and use their abilities to enhance the lives of others and the profession. They consistently seek to expand their ability to help more people or help them more effectively.

Plagiarism

Intentional plagiarism is a form of intellectual theft that violates widely recognized principles of academic integrity as well as the Honor Code. Such plagiarism may subject the student to appropriate disciplinary action administered through the university Honor Code Office, in addition to academic sanctions that may be applied by an instructor. Inadvertent plagiarism, which may not be a violation of the Honor Code, is nevertheless a form of intellectual carelessness that is unacceptable in the academic community. Plagiarism of any kind is completely contrary to the established practices of higher education where all members of the university are expected to acknowledge the original intellectual work of others that is included in their own work. In some cases, plagiarism may also involve violations of copyright law. Intentional Plagiarism-Intentional plagiarism is the deliberate act of representing the words, ideas, or data of another as one’s own without providing proper attribution to the author through quotation, reference, or footnote. Inadvertent Plagiarism-Inadvertent plagiarism involves the inappropriate, but non-deliberate, use of another’s words, ideas, or data without proper attribution. Inadvertent plagiarism usually results from an ignorant failure to follow established rules for documenting sources or from simply not being sufficiently careful in research and writing. Although not a violation of the Honor Code, inadvertent plagiarism is a form of academic misconduct for which an instructor can impose appropriate academic sanctions. Students who are in doubt as to whether they are providing proper attribution have the responsibility to consult with their instructor and obtain guidance. Examples of plagiarism include: Direct Plagiarism-The verbatim copying of an original source without acknowledging the source. Paraphrased Plagiarism-The paraphrasing, without acknowledgement, of ideas from another that the reader might mistake for the author’s own. Plagiarism Mosaic-The borrowing of words, ideas, or data from an original source and blending this original material with one’s own without acknowledging the source. Insufficient Acknowledgement-The partial or incomplete attribution of words, ideas, or data from an original source. Plagiarism may occur with respect to unpublished as well as published material. Copying another student’s work and submitting it as one’s own individual work without proper attribution is a serious form of plagiarism.
