Prospective Undergraduate Researchers

Below are some suggestions on how to get involved in our lab. These are just guidelines, and sometimes vary depending on the prior experience level and motivation of the person and the current needs and funding levels of the project.

Prerequisites:

  • ME 273 (always)
  • ME 312 (usually)

First Steps (~5 hrs/week for one semester):

  • Read about ongoing research projects and review related publications.
  • Attend our group meetings to learn more about current projects and meet some of the other students (currently Thursdays at 2PM in 431A CTB).
  • Visit with one or more of my graduate students in an area of interest to discuss how you might assist on their team. Ask for suggestions for what you need to learn. The things you need to learn differ by project, which is why I can’t make a universal list but generally includes: Python or Julia, Unix command line, git, project specific reading (journal articles and textbooks), and project specific tools like OpenMDAO or Star-CCM+.
  • Volunteer some time working on small tasks with a graduate student and getting up to speed on the recommended tools/concepts. Proceed to next steps once you’ve learned some foundational skills and we (you, graduate student mentor, I) have identified a project would be a good fit for a semester-length undergraduate project.

Next Steps (~10 hrs/week for one semester):

  • Send me a resume and statement of interest.
  • Meet with me to setup a ME 497R where you can receive three elective credits working on a semester-length research project.
  • Continue learning about high performance scientific computing: Coursera course, course notes
  • Continue learning about aerodynamics (Hansen textbook for Wind, Anderson textbook for Flight).
  • Meet weekly with your research team and graduate student mentor to report progress and discuss ideas.

Research Assistant (10-20 hrs/week on a continuing basis):

  • With mutual interest (and sufficient funding) you will then be hired as a paid research assistant and expected to work towards high impact publications in collaboration with a graduate student(s). In some cases you may lead a project, particularly if expecting to transition into a graduate student position within our group.

New Graduate Students

Your goal as a researcher is to make an impact for good in the world and improve the way we design wind and flight systems. The primary way to make an impact is through tackling challenging and important questions, performing high-quality insightful work, collaborating with industry and other researchers, and sharing findings broadly at conferences and in peer-reviewed journals.

Open Source Codes

  • Akima a 1D Akima spline interpolation method with analytic derivatives (Python/Fortran)
  • Tools we developed for NREL WISDEM, which is a larger set of models designed for assessing wind plant cost of energy. All of these are written in Python (some are Fortran, C, or C++ wrapped in Python) and implemented in the OpenMDAO framework.
    • CCBlade: a blade-element momentum method with guaranteed convergence behavior (beneficial for gradient-based optimization).
    • pBEAM: a beam finite element method.
    • AirfoilPreppy: a set of utilities for preprocessing airfoil aerodynamic data.
    • pyFrame3DD: a wrapper to an existing finite element code with a few custom additions.
    • RotorSE: a wind turbine rotor analysis module coupleing CCBlade, pBEAM, PreComp and other methods for rotor aero/structural analysis.
    • TowerSE: a wind turbine tower analysis module using pBEAM, cylinder drag theory, linear wave theory, and methods from Eurocode and GL.
    • TurbineSE: a wind turbine module integrating RotorSE, NacelleSE, and TowerSE
    • LandBOS: a C/Python implementation of a balance-of-station cost model.
    • CommonSE: a collection of common utilities shared amongst the WISDEM modules.
    • FLORISSE, a HAWT wake code with analytic gradients
